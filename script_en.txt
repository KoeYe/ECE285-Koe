Quick ref: PSNR 34.66 dB / SSIM 0.9959 / Drift_10 0.023 / Planning SR both 5%

SLIDE 1 — Title
We're working on action-conditioned world modeling —
given a frame and an action, predict what the next frame looks like.

SLIDE 2 — The Problem
Existing approaches are at two extremes.
Pixel-space models lack semantic structure; pure latent models can't decode back to pixels.
We sit in the middle: a latent token block that a Transformer generates
and a U-Net directly decodes into a pixel prediction.

SLIDE 3 — Architecture
Four components: a CNN encoder, an action embedding,
a 4-layer Transformer predictor that outputs K=8 latent tokens,
and a U-Net decoder that maps those tokens back to a 64x64 RGB frame.
Total: 5.8M parameters.

SLIDE 4 — Three-Stage Training
We decompose training into three stages.
Stage 0 warms up the decoder on the FrozenLake visual domain — gets us to 31.3 dB.
Stage 1 freezes everything except the predictor and aligns its latent output to
the encoder's real latents — cosine similarity hits 0.997.
Stage 2 trains end-to-end with an embedding regularization term — final PSNR 34.66 dB.

SLIDE 5 — Setup
FrozenLake, deterministic, custom 64x64 renderer.
200 random maps, 148K transitions, single L40S GPU, under 3 hours total.

SLIDE 6 — Main Results
PSNR 34.66 dB, up 3.36 from our baseline.
SSIM 0.9959 — near-perfect structural match.
Drift_10 is 0.023, so quality holds up across 10 auto-regressive steps.
The embedding loss is what keeps rollouts stable —
it regularizes in feature space, not just pixels.

SLIDE 7 — Rollout Visualization
Ten auto-regressive steps, no ground truth feedback.
Each row: GT, prediction, error ×10.
Agent position and tile colors track well through step 8;
errors cluster at holes and boundaries.

SLIDE 8 — Ablation
Four ablations.
No Stage 0 collapses to 9.45 dB — the decoder needs its visual prior.
No embedding loss keeps one-step PSNR similar but multi-step drift worsens 65%.
K=8 is the sweet spot over K=2/4/16.
Unfreezing the decoder inflates PSNR to 49 dB, but it's a degenerate solution
— the model just memorizes latents instead of learning dynamics.

SLIDE 9 & 10 — Latent Space
PCA of the predicted latents shows four clean clusters, one per action.
Each cluster splits into two sub-clouds: effective transitions vs. boundary no-ops
where the agent hits a wall and nothing changes.
This structure emerges with no explicit supervision —
the decoder forces the predictor to separate them.

SLIDE 11 — Key Insights
Three takeaways: the embedding loss acts as a stable attractor for rollouts,
freezing the decoder is an inductive bias not a limitation,
and staged training makes an otherwise intractable optimization tractable.

SLIDE 12 — Planning
We pair the world model with a LoRA fine-tuned Qwen2-VL-2B for a planning experiment.
The integration is a lookahead-consistency filter:
the WM predicts next frames for all 4 actions,
the VLM re-evaluates from each, and we prune actions that want to immediately reverse.
Result: VLM-only 5%, VLM+WM 5% — they tie.
Single-step lookahead is still greedy search; it can't escape local dead ends.
High prediction quality does not automatically translate to planning gains —
you need multi-step rollout with a value function.

SLIDE 13 — Conclusion
Three-stage latent world model, 5.8M params, single GPU, under 3 hours.
PSNR 34.66, stable multi-step rollouts.
Staged training and embedding regularization are the two key design choices.
Next steps: scheduled sampling, stochastic environments,
and multi-step planning with a learned value function.
